Student Advice Study Guide
  • What calculator do I need? The exam is mostly terms and concepts with very few calculations. You do NOT need to buy an expensive calculator to take the exam.
  • Overview: The course covers introductory undergraduate statistical concepts. So you'll need to assess your basic stats knowledge; i.e. have you had a stats course, was it recent, and more importantly, do you TRULY understand the basic concepts.
  • About the Author: This student advice guide, is written from the perspective of someone who had a stats course within the last 3-years. However, it was an online course that you could pass the homework and exams with good regurgitation of formulas. So this time around, I wanted to actually learn stats; a novel concept, I know.
  • What calculator do I need: The exam is mostly terms and concepts with very few calculations. You do NOT need to buy an expensive calculator to take the exam. After all, our degree is about learning how-to use statistical software to do calculations. So you won't be doing much math by hand/calculator! The exam has a built-in calculator with the addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division function. It gets the job done, although a squared and factorial function would be nice. I used a scientific calculator I had laying around the house from the Dollar Tree, that cost me $1. Go to Course Tips to review non-permitted calculators. But I say, you don't need a calculator that cost any more than $10 or $20. Seriously, don't invest a lot of money in a calculator, that's what the statistical software is for!
  • What books do I need: Also, I wouldn't invest a lot of money in books. In the world of open courses, there are more than enough FREE resources, to not only help you pass the exam, but mastery introductory statistics. Which I will provide a study guide at the end. If you insist on purchasing a book, then I recommend going to your local library first and check-out all their stats books. What you'll find is, all books aren't created equal. Some are more "user friendly" than others. Also, all books don't cover the topics you need. For example, it will cover descriptive stats (measure of center, measure of spread, graphical displays, etc.), but fall short on inferential statistics (z-score, t-test, f-test, etc.). Hint: the exam focuses on the latter, so make sure you get a book that covers those topics. So it's important you start with your local library or local book store, where you can review the book before you buy it. I did get 5+ books from my library, a few were decent, but none I felt gave me an "edge" over a free resource and I "had" to have in my personal library. I'm not against you purchasing a book, but just make sure it brings you value.
  • What do I need to read in zyBooks: On the topic of books, it's important to know, zyBooks is NOT a primary study resource. I'm not saying it's a bad book, but it's less a book for someone with zero knowledge and more a REVIEW guide that will help prepare you for the exam. I learned that early by reading the discussion forums. I also can confirm, because I tested reading a chapter in zyBooks first compared to reading a chapter in zyBooks after I read my primary study material. It was like first compared to reading a chapter in zyBooks after I read my primary study material. It was like night and day differences in understanding. An analogy I will use is like driving a car. Do you need to know what's going on under the hood to be able to drive a car, no. But if your profession is a mechanic do you need to know? Um... it's probably a good idea! Our profession is data analytics, which is built on the foundation of statistics. Yes, statistical software will do much of the manual calculation for us. But if the car starts making a funny clunking noise, then people expect us to know what's going on and how-to fix. So it's important we do less to memorize formulas and more to understand how said formulas are derived. That's my 1-minute rant, to really taking this course serious and learning the foundations of what our degree is built-on. Back on the topic of zyBooks. Skim at the text, review all the interactive activities, and do the quizzes to make sure you have a basic understanding of what's it trying to teach. Also, per forum discussion, some of the math problems are wrong in zyBooks, so be on the lookout for that. What to know before you take the exam: If you feel you understand basic stats concepts, then I would go through zyBooks as a review, take the practice exam, and then exam. If you're starting from the beginning, then I would review your primary study resource, review zyBooks, take practice exam 1, repeat first two steps for concepts you missed on the practice exam, take practice exam 2, repeat first two steps for concepts you missed on the practice exam, take practice exam 3, then exam. Either way, I would save your first practice exam until after you've at least completed 50% of your studying, but before you've completed 80% of your studying. I say 50%, because I don't want you to waste your practice exam attempts. And I say 80%, because I don't want you to get "stuck" over studying; you need to take a practice exam to gauge your study efforts. Also, under Course Information section, there is a first attempt checklist, that says much of the same thing.
  • What to expect on the exam: Now I know I said zyBooks is not a primary study resource, but it is THEE resource for the exam. Meaning, if you compare 3-stats book side-by-side, sure they'll be "similar." But they may call the same concept a different term or they may choose to include or exclude certain lessor used concepts. This tripped me up on the first practice exam. Different terms were used, I over studied certain concepts, while not studying others, etc. So if you're forming your own study guide, make sure you form it using zyBooks as an outline. Also, use zyBooks as a review, so you can familiarize yourself to the terms used on the exam (i.e. independent and dependent variables matches to explanatory and response variables, etc.).
  • What's the best way to study statistics: On the topic of zyBooks outline. Obvious chapter one and nine, you can toss out. Chapter two, descriptive statistics, is not directly on the exam, but you're expected to know the concepts; plus it's just a review of C740. So that leaves chapter 3-8, which I do NOT feel is the best order. I understand why they broke it out like they did, but I disagree from a learning perspective that it makes the most sense. I had to re-read and re-watch many videos, because I didn't understand how each concept in stats related to the other. You will be a lot better student, if you understand how each concept builds on the previous. For example, probability is not covered in this course, but it's probability concepts that bridges descriptive statistics with inferential statistics. By providing us the mathematical proofs for the normal distribution curve and bring meaning to p-values; otherwise it just seems like random numbers out of the sky. So whatever study guide you create, make sure it's one that finds meaning and connects a new concept to a previous concept. Also, make sure it's one that focuses less on math/formulas and more on why/how the formulas are derived. After all, memorizing formulas does you no good, if you use the wrong statistical test or make the wrong interpretation. Statistical software will "memorize" formulas and do the mundane calculation for you, our job/value is in knowing how-to ask the right questions, setting up the correct testing methodology, and making the right interpretations from the data. Okay... I promise that's the last rant on the differences between regurgitation vs learning!

  • Exam Day: I would say once you're scoring 75-80% on the practice test you're ready. The practice exam was good prep for the real exam, because it gives you a good idea of what to study. Some concepts were less emphasized and others more, but if you studied to learn, then you should be prepared. My final score resembled my 2 practice exam attempt. This makes sense because your first attempt you're just getting accumulated and 3rd attempt you've probably memorized the answers. I didn't feel at a disadvantage with my $1 scientific calculator; matter fact there was even less calculations on the exam. When you schedule your exam set it for a minimum 30-45 minutes in the future. This gives you time to read the policies in the testing portal and take a test proctor exam. If this is your first WGU objective exam, I highly recommended it, because it will help alleviate unneeded stress. Here you download the needed software, go through a practice proctor check-in process, move items off your desk, etc. Then you still have about 5-10 minutes to re-center your balance. Last, there's no breaks on this exam. So make sure you take your bathroom break, get your water/liquid motivation, grab your Kleenex, or anything else you think you might need before!
  • Anything else: Most important, do NOT get discouraged; especially if this is your first time taking stats or it's been a while. Keep in mind, you're trying to cram 16-weeks of a traditional undergrad course into 2-8 weeks. So it can and will be overwhelming at times. Just remember, your family believes in you and your WGU family believes in you, so do your best and forget the rest!
